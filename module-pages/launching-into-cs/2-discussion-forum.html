Unit 1 Discussion Forum

This was the first discussion forum task of the module. It involves making a post in Unit 1, then commenting on other students' posts through units 2 and 3. The brief was as follows:

Download and Read Data protection rules as a trust-enabler in the EU and beyond - taking stock (COM/2019/374)' Available from: European Union Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) of personal data inside and outside the EU.

Also read the GDPR Regulation at: Regulation (EU) 2016/679.

Now review your (or an) organisation's IT Code of Conduct and reflect on:

  • Best practices.
  • Areas that can be improved.
  • Incidents (anonymously) and your role as a computing professional.
  • What could have been done to improve the situation.

My Initial Post

Computer systems in some form are used by billions of people around the world on a daily basis. The scale of this utility places a significant burden of responsibility upon both the users and the professionals who develop, maintain and deliver these systems.

The British Computer Society outline four major points in their code of conduct, intended as an outline of ethical and professional practice for all computing professionals (BCS, 2022):

  • 'Public interest'
  • 'Professional competency and integrity'
  • 'Duty to relevant authority'
  • 'Duty to the profession'
These tenets serve the purpose of providing broad guidelines against which IT professionals can make decisions on their own professional conduct. The prevalent theme is one of ethical behaviour and awareness of the wider implications of the actions of the individual. Somewhat counter-intuitively when considering a technical field, the BCS code of conduct suggests that best practice for IT professionals is focused more on behaviour, integrity and respect than on specific technical practices. This approach is shared by similar organisations too. The Association for Computing Machinery Code of Ethics (ACM, 2018), whilst more detailed, has the same emphasis on the importance of ethical behaviour for computing professionals.

Whilst the purpose of the BCS code of conduct and ACM code of ethics is to enable professionals to make their own ethical decisions, it does leave significant room for interpretation and degree of application at an individual level. Also, whilst these documents are indicative of the values which professional bodies consider to be of high importance for individuals working in the field, they are not directly enforceable for those who are not members of the respective organisation. This emphasises the importance of enacting more focused codes of conduct for organisations, which relate to their specific activities, and also the importance of regulation, which protects good, ethical practice in law.

Looking at the IT policy enforced by my employer, I can see that the focus shifts considerably towards security, as opposed to ethics. The policy covers physical security, such as not leaving equipment unattended or using USB drives, as well as digital security related to downloading non-approved software or enforcing the use of multi-factor authentication for all devices. Whilst there is some focus on ethical use of computer equipment, it seems to be driven by protecting corporate reputation and intellectual property as opposed to being for the greater good, as seen in the codes of conduct from the BCS and ACM. However, the company is also protecting itself from penalties enacted as a result of failing to adhere to regulations (such as GDPR) which in turn, protect individuals with whom the company conducts business (Regulation (EU) 2016/679 of the European Parliament and of the Council of 27 April 2016 on the protection of natural persons with regard to the processing of personal data and on the free movement of such data, and repealing Directive 95/46/EC (General Data Protection Regulation), 2016).

I have found in the past that corporate IT policy can prove to be cumbersome in practice, as the policies are often strict and prevent necessary functionality for people within certain job roles. I believe this is an unfortunate side-effect of trying to apply a single policy to every area of the business, which is generally diverse in regards to awareness of threats and computer literacy. This perceived inconvenience can and does lead to employees willingly disregarding the policy, which can in turn damage both the company and individuals whose personal data may be leaked or stolen without their knowledge.

Initially, I considered that implementing a more versatile and less impactful IT code of conduct, whilst being more laborious for the company, would yield an improvement on the problem of employees willingly disregarding the policy. However, a recent investigation on a number of employees at large organisations showed that the employees perception of how much or little the policy inconvenienced them did not seem to influence how obedient they were of the policy (Kim & Han, 2018). Interestingly, the same investigation found a positive correlation between employee compliance to the IT policy and the employee's perception of how ethical the company was. Considering my own evaluation of my employer's policy to be more focused on security than ethics, I found it interesting that this investigation brought the focus of conduct, even for corporate policies, back to ethics.

The differences in codes of conduct between organisations that I have considered above seems sensible. Professional bodies will have codes of conduct which protect the profession, and companies will have codes of conduct which protect the company. Regulation will drive these policies, as often penalties from non-compliance to regulation are what organisations (companies more so) are protecting against. Consistently, ethical behaviour has underpinned the content of these codes of conduct, either directly or indirectly. This particularly makes sense for computer science, where many of the technologies in use are either transient or very new. Much of the discourse focuses on specific ethical practices such as professionalism, continuous learning and honesty, particularly as related to an individual's competency (Stahl, Timmermans & Mittelstadt, 2016). By associating good practice so directly with these ethical behaviours, the complexity of maintaining a good code of conduct for computer professionals is considerably reduced by eliminating dependency on any specific technology, which can be enforced at an organisational level, and encourages a culture of working to values which are beneficial for society as a whole.
