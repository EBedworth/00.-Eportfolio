Blog Post

For Unit 2, we were asked to write a 300-word blog post on how people can be managed to overcome cyber security attacks from the inside, based on five words which we would select from the ISO/IEC 27000 Section 3 - Terms and Definitions. My submitted blog post can be seen below.

Fighting cyber security threats through management of the people who introduce them


Friday, 17 November 2023, 6:28 PM by Elliot Bedworth


Cyber security is a constant, ongoing threat to organisations of all sizes. Whilst prioritisation of cyber security has generally increased over the last decade (Department for Science, Innovation and Technology, 2023), the ever-evolving nature of cyber security means that threats continue to exist.

Whilst technological challenges evolve, the part that people play remains, to some degree, a consistent part of the cyber security chain. Where some research has tried to quantify the risk introduced through human factors on cyber security (Evans et al., 2016), very many sources agree on the significant role which people play in the introduction of cyber security risks.

Below, we will explore some of the terms from ISO/IEC 27000:2018, relating to information security techniques, and see how they might be applied to the management of people in order to mitigate some of the cyber security risks which organisations face.

Organisation

Organisations of all different sizes and structures face cyber security challenges (Department for Science, Innovation and Technology, 2023). As such, there is likely no 'one size fits all' solution in regards to organisational changes which can impact the human factors of cyber security. However, there are still common recommendations which transcend size or structure. Some research has found that factors such as the perceived level of ethics of an organisation can increase the likelihood that an employee will comply with IT policy and thus reduce the chance of that employee introducing risk (Kim & Han, 2018).

Policy

Employee compliance to IT policy is not valuable, unless the policy is well designed. Some areas of focus here should be on the indirect aspects of such a policy: how it relates to the employees and the long-term goals achieved by implementing such a policy (Höne & Eloff, 2022).

Top Management

Cultural shifts within a business are most effectively conducted when delivered from the top. Although a large proportion of businesses have adopted the approach of assigning specific, director-level leaders as responsible for cyber security, the level of buy-in should be extended further still to include the entire board as equally responsible and knowledgeable about the threats which exist.

Continual Improvement

To stand a chance against the changing state-of-play that is cyber security, individuals must accept a culture of continual improvement and be motivated to complete training and learn the new risks which arise in a timely fashion. This responsibility ultimately lies with the organisations who demand the training be completed, who must ensure that employees do not disengage through such phenomena as 'Cyber Fatigue' (Reeves et al., 2021).

Review

None of the above steps are possible without effective review and reflection. As with any formal learning, review is a crucial stage in ensuring that people, policy and practice are current and fit for purpose. Reviews should be organised and undertaken at regular, defined intervals against a set of criteria which allow objective and useful revision to take place.
