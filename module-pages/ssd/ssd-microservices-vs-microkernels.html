Microservices vs Microkernels Debate

In Unit 11, we were asked to pick a side in the 'Microservices vs Microkernels' debate (DiBona & Ockman, 1999) and post our justification. In Unit 12, we had to defend our position against an opposing one.

My Position

I made the following post in the forum regarding the Microservices vs Microkernels debate:

Why Torvalds is still not completely wrong

by Elliot Bedworth - Sunday, 18 February 2024, 4:50 PM

Although he point being made that 'microservices and microkernels are the future' may appear to be correct, the prevalence of microservice architecture does not not exist exclusively from monolithic architecture.

Driven by the increasing demand to maintain large software systems across diverse teams, the application of microservices in software development can be considered a modernisation of legacy application development (Escobar et al., 2016). This trend towards creating an increased amount of smaller components is also observed in other areas of software development, such as in the object-oriented programming paradigm, or through the usage of Agile workflows (Chen et al., 2017). It is logical, then, that more frequent use of a microservice or microkernel architecture should be observed, as the working practices of software teams develop in a similar way.

There are few advantages that could reasonably be argued for monolithic architecture over microservice architecture, however, those few will still serve niche applications where they apply. For very simple, isolated systems, monolithic architecture remains easy (and cheap) to design, build and run for a long period of time. For applications where interfacing to external services is not possible, monolithic architecture may remain the only choice, something which is also prevalent in certain areas of industry.

I believe that whilst a continuing increase in the application of microservice and microkernel architecture is likely, this does not mean that monolithic architecture will be completely replaced. It should also be considered, in the context of the debate, that Linux is still a widely used operating system whilst remaining monolithic. I believe the idea of coexistence is too quickly dismissed by agreeing with either side of this debate, and think that this is the most likely outcome for the future.

References

Chen, R., Li, S. and Li, Z. (2017) Monolith to microservices: A dataflow-driven approach. 2017 24th Asia-Pacific Software Engineering Conference (APSEC): 466-475.

Escobar, D., Cárdenas, D., Amarillo, R., Castro, E., Garcés, K., Parra, C. & Casallas, R. (2016) Towards the understanding and evolution of monolithic applications as microservices. 2016 XLII Latin American computing conference (CLEI): 1-11.

Team Position

As a team, we had very similar positions on this argument even before discussing with each other. We all felt that microservice architecture is prevalent as the architecture of choice for the majority of new systems, however, we also do not think that this will lead to the extinction of monolithic systems. As discussed in my own post, there still exists some niche use-cases for monolithic systems. Looking to Linux as an example of such a system, the wide existing usage of Linux is unlikely to disappear, and although it is possible that Linux is 'modernised' into a microservice architecture, we believe that such systems will never be completely 'lost' to those systems which surplant them.

Although there were no opposing arguments in the forum, I would argue that it is an oversimplification to claim that because one architecture has overtaken the other in popularity, that the other architecture is obsolete and has no future.
